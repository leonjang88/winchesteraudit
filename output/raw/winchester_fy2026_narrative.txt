SUBJECT: Town Manager’s FY 2027 Financial Plan
Pursuant to Chapter 4, Section 1.2 of the Winchester Code of Bylaws, I present the proposed
budget for Fiscal Year 2027.
Since the adoption of the FY 2026 budget by Town Meeting in May of 2025, the Winchester
community has been engaged in an intensive analysis of short- and long-term municipal
finances and how best to address both rising operational costs as well as ways to
incorporate important new initiatives into the budget. Known as the State of the Town, this
working group represents a cross-section of Winchester’s elected and appointed leadership.
Working with Town staff, this group produced a wealth of information on everything from
capital budgeting to educational priorities to non-property tax revenues. The State of the
Town consensus reached after months of discussion is that seven years after the adoption
of the last Proposition 2 ½ operating override in 2019, it is time to present Winchester voters
with another operating override question in 2026. On February 12, 2026, the Select Board
certified such a question for the ballot for the March 21, 2026, Town Election.
Under Winchester’s Bylaws, this proposed budget must be presented in February, a month
before the override question reaches the ballot. Therefore, this budget must be built based
on the fiscal realities of the moment, not on any potential override. The Finance Committee
will take up the budget process next and will be working over the next several months on
developing an FY27 budget to present to Town Meeting. During this time FinCom will have
the opportunity to make changes to the budget based on new information, and I and other
staff will work closely with them as they deliberate.
So, with that preface, let me present the proposed FY 27 Winchester Budget.
The Budget Process
Winchester is governed by a Town Charter, approved in 1975, which designates a five-
member elected Select Board as the executive branch, a representative Town Meeting of 192
1

=== Page 5 | Unknown ===
members, 24 elected from each of eight precincts, as the legislative branch, and a
professional manager appointed by the Select Board to serve as the Chief Administrative
Officer. It is the Town Manager’s responsibility under the Charter to prepare a proposed
annual budget, which is then presented to the Finance Committee in February for public
review and deliberation. This review generally takes place from February through April, with
the Finance Committee’s budget presented to Town Meeting for adoption in May.
Town staff begin preparing their budgets in the fall, with meetings taking place into
December as a final proposed budget takes shape. In January the Town receives updates on
the cost of benefits and insurance, as well as the proposed costs and revenue sharing in the
Governor’s fiscal year budget (with the largest single items being Chapter 70 School Aid and
Unrestricted General Government Aid).
This year, following the year-long community conversation led by the State of the Town
working group, the Select Board voted to instruct the Town Manager to limit the use of free
cash in developing the FY27 budget. The vote allowed for the use of $2.1 million in projected
FY26 turnbacks/surplus revenue (as projected by the State of the Town model), with a cap
on the use of additional free cash to a range of not more than $3 - $4 million. Following that
directive, the proposed total budget figure presented here includes the $2.1 million of
turnbacks/surplus revenue projected from FY26 as well as $3 million in additional free cash.
The Proposed Budget
The proposed FY27 budget totals $171,733,429 across all departments and expense lines,
which represents a 3.18% increase from FY26. It will require that $1,737,059 be cut from the
proposed budgets, shared proportionately by the municipal, school and undistributed
budgets as noted in the budget summary in the next section. Addressing these gaps will
require additional conversations in the weeks ahead.
Among the highlights:
Revenue
- Property tax remains the Town’s primary revenue source. The property tax levy is
permitted to increase 2.5% over the prior year, plus the taxable value of new
construction, also known as new growth. Prior year new growth becomes part of
the tax base, and FY26’s new growth added into next year’s base brings the FY27
levy increase to a total of 3.77% (this does not include debt exclusions,
capital/building reserve funds, or Chapter 110)
- New growth: Based on the recommendation of the Director of Assessments, the
new growth projection for FY27 is $750,000, reduced from the FY26 actual figure
of $1,302,085.
- State aid: The Governor’s proposed FY27 budget allocates a total of $13,800,793
in state aid to Winchester, up 2.71% from FY26. These preliminary figures are

As noted above, this proposed budget includes unallocated reductions spread among the
three primary cost centers – municipal, schools, and unallocated. The figures are identified
in red on the spreadsheet which follows. Outlines of how these gaps would be filled were
presented at the Special Town Meeting of February 9, 2026. Further conversations will take

The Financial Environment
Federal cuts, particularly to research grants and health care, have placed increasing
pressure on Beacon Hill lawmakers as they develop the FY27 State Budget. While the
Governor’s budget contains no cuts to local aid, it falls short of addressing sharply increased
costs impacting both general municipal and school budgets, including health care expenses
and special education, to name two of the most significant.
Despite these fiscal challenges, Winchester’s financial foundation remains strong. Moody’s
has assigned Winchester a AAA rating, most recently affirmed in a February 2025 report. This
3

=== Page 7 | Unknown ===
rating provides significant savings when the Town borrows for long-term capital investments,
including the recently completed Lynch Elementary School and a host of smaller drainage
and other flood mitigation projects. Moody’s next rating update will be released later this
February.
Looking Ahead
Winchester went into the FY26 budget process with a $3.9 million deficit, funded with $3.5
million of free cash and $400,000 of the overlay surplus. While this filled the budget gap, it
did not correct the underlying expense and revenue imbalance.
Planning for the FY27 budget started with an even larger imbalance, fueled by the ever-
increasing costs of employee benefits, as well as contractual settlements necessary to
retain and maintain a strong professional staff across school and municipal operations.
Winchester’s budget remains highly dependent on the real property tax, which covered
roughly 73% of the FY26 budget (including debt exclusions for capital projects). Some 95%
of that number is generated by residential property. State aid and local receipts combine to
cover about 15% of the budget.
As part of the effort to help ease some of that burden, the Select Board has charged the Town
Manager’s office with developing a plan to generate more non-property tax revenue. This
budget reflects first steps in these areas, with the first new building and zoning fee schedule
since 2021 heading for the Select Board, and updated revenue targets for investment income
being implemented. Work will continue in the months ahead on these and other efforts to
find new non-property tax revenue.
The Select Board has also directed the Town Manager’s office to look at ways to address
current and future employee benefit costs. With Winchester on target to fund its accrued
pension obligations in FY28 (ahead of the state mandated deadline), funding the future costs
of retiree health coverage remains an ever-growing challenge.
Conclusion
Winchester’s tradition of making investments in its people, its programs and services, and
its critical infrastructure are central to the community’s success. As discussions begin on
this proposal and residents address longer-term financial decisions, these traditions anchor
Winchester’s fiscal foundation.

detailed in the Town Charter, first adopted in 1975. The Manager is responsible for the day-

Program Description
The Finance Committee consists of fifteen voters appointed for staggered three-year terms.

and the Chairman of the Select Board and Finance Committee respectively. Under the
provision of the Town Charter and ByLaws, the Finance Committee receives and reviews the
proposed budget submitted by the Town Manager, and after public hearings, submits a
budget to the Town Meeting. The ongoing function of the Committee is to act upon requests
for “transfers”; i.e., appropriations from the Reserve Fund to meet extraordinary and
unforeseen needs of town departments and agencies which may arise during the course of
the fiscal year.

Program Description
The Comptroller is appointed by the Select Board and is responsible by Town Charter and State
statute for maintaining the general and budgetary accounting records of the Town, preparing the
weekly payroll and accounts payable warrants, preparing various financial reports of a statutory
and/or administrative nature, developing methods of installation and supervision of all accounting
records of the Town, developing standard practices relating to account matters, and the continuously
reconciling, reviewing and auditing account balances and activity. The Comptroller provides budget
and forecast planning support during the budget development process and as needed.
The Comptroller’s Office administers the unemployment activity and the general insurance program
which includes property, liability (and excess liability), public official, flood, vacancy and other
coverage.
In addition to the day-to-day duties and responsibilities, the Comptroller’s Office is responsible for
preparing the Department of Revenue Division of Local Services (DLS) reports and data for the Free
Cash certification, preparing/coordinating the DLS tax recapitulation for tax rate certification,
preparing and filing IRS required forms (employee W-2’s, vendor 1099’s and Affordable Care Act
1095c forms) annually. The Comptroller’s Office also coordinates the annual financial statement
audit, which includes providing a myriad of supporting documentation and analysis and coordinates
the Other Post-Employment Benefits (OPEB) actuarial studies and roll-forwards, which includes
preparing complex insurance and retiree census data.
The Comptroller is the ex-officio voting member of the Winchester Contributory Retirement Board,
an ex-officio non-voting member of the Commissioners of Trust Funds and an ex-officio non-voting
member of the Town’s Retiree Health Insurance Advisory Committee.
FY2027 Initiatives
• Continue meeting internal and external deadlines.
• Continue to find ways to make operations more effective and efficient.
• Continue to provide training and other assistance associated with the accounting software.
• Continue to explore new programs in the accounting software to utilize.
• Implement more facets of the Tyler Content Manager program.
• Collaborate with Town Management to update the Financial Policies and Procedures
Manual.
• Collaborate with Town Management to develop a formal risk assessment policy.

Program Description
The Audit budget provides funding for the annual financial statement audit performed in
accordance with generally accepted auditing standards and standards applicable to
financial audits contained in Government Auditing Standards. A financial statement audit is
an examination of the Town’s books and records and includes the review of accounting
transactions, policies, procedures, and internal controls. The Town is required to obtain a
financial statement audit due to the level of federal funds expended and due to bond
disclosure requirements.
The Select Board established an Audit Advisory Committee in 2006 to assist them in their
oversight of the annual financial statement audit. The Audit Advisory Committee facilitates
communication between the Select Board, the audit firm and town management. They also
make recommendations related to the selection of the audit firm and review the financial
statements and other audit reports regarding financial management issues. The Comptroller
is responsible for the day-to-day management of the audit process and works closely with
the audit firm. Fiscal Year 2027 is the first year of a new audit contract which is estimated to
cost $90,000. The Audit Advisory Committee has recommended to continue the
relationship with CliftonLarsonAllen, LLP.
The audit budget also provides funding for a biennial Other Post Employment Benefit (OPEB)
actuarial valuation and consulting services. The Town currently provides postemployment
benefits including medical, dental and life insurance to retirees and their dependents. The
actuarial valuation is required to provide the necessary data associated with these benefits
to comply with financial statement reporting and disclosure requirements in accordance
with Governmental Accounting Standards Board Statements No.74 & 75. There will not be
an actuarial valuation performed in FY26, but there will be one in FY27, so funds were added
in the FY27 budget

Program Description
The Conservation Commission is appointed by the Town Manager and is responsible for
protecting the Town’s natural resources. The Conservation Commission manages over
130+ acres of open space, dedicated to passive recreation and to the preservation of
wildlife habitat. These areas give quiet refuge and an opportunity to experience nature and
serve as habitat for wildlife. We do this on a small budget, making use of much volunteer
help, both with Commission members and volunteers who donate their time to clear
passages, build crossings, construct signs, and blaze trails. With each permit issued, we
educate landowners in the importance of food and cover to native and migrating species
and require them to landscape for their preservation. We are actively involved in promoting
clean water, and work to ensure that discharges into waterbodies are free of pollutants. We
have watched waterfowl and fish return to our waterways, the return of migrating herring,
and the return of apex species - indicative of a healthy ecosystem. In the long term, it is
these preserved areas, and the efforts of educated landowners, that will contribute to flood
resilience, drought control, and a quality of life that incorporates nature and its many
benefits.
FY2027 Initiatives
• Establish and maintain trails, boardwalks and bridges in Conservation Area that
enables the use of these spaces,
• Remove invasive species and repopulate native plants in conservation and open
spaces to increase biodiversity, promote pollinators, and improve wildlife habitat,
• Restore the public shoreline of Winchester’s Great Ponds: Wedge Pond and Winter
Pond,
• Manage invasive aquatic species in Winchester water bodies to restore the natural
resource and provide wildlife habitat,
• Work in conjunction with the Town to improve water quality in the Aberjona River,
Wedge Pond, and Winter Pond to restore the natural resources, improve resident’s
health and well-being and provide wildlife habitat.

the community and the Commonwealth by providing innovative, efficient, quality service.

through fair, open, transparent, and consistent administration of all federal, state, and local
election laws. The Town Clerk provides educational opportunities to those residents and
voters who may be interested in the structure of our town government and its electoral
process. Training opportunities are available to those voters in Winchester who would like to

Program Description
The Winchester Public Library is a dynamic community resource that endeavors to improve
the quality of life for Library patrons of all ages by providing:
● the resources, services and support they need to make informed decisions, resolve
issues or answer questions throughout life.
● children and teens with the programs, services, and materials they need to succeed
in school and satisfy their educational and recreational interests as they grow from
infants to young adults.
● a safe and welcoming physical space to meet and interact with others or to sit quietly
and read and have access to the ever-growing resources and services available
through the library’s web site.
To accomplish these endeavors and more, the Library relies upon a professional staff, a
broad collection of current materials in a variety of formats, current information technology,
and a well-maintained facility for patrons.
The Winchester Public Library is under the jurisdiction of a five-member elected Board of
Trustees.

from Sage Consulting. There will be several ways for residents to participate in the planning
process, including focus groups and a community survey in early 2026. The 3-year plan is
projected to be completed by late spring 2026 and will guide next steps and initiatives.
There have been challenges in the past year, and moving forward, more staff (including
serving teens and also custodial staff hours) are needed to continue to provide a safe and
comfortable environment to all.

and dining, transportation services, educational programs and events, information and
referral to access resources. A majority of clients are age 75 and older and most are women.
The COA continues to serve a growing population of Mandarin-speaking older adults, over

Program Description
The Police Department’s primary function is the enforcement of laws and protection of life
and property. This includes prevention of crime, preservation of the peace, law enforcement
and detection of violators. When law violators are detected, they can be cited, summonsed
or arrested. In the case of juveniles and young adults, when appropriate the police
department can utilize various diversion programs to prevent a juvenile or young adult from
having a criminal record. The Police Department responds to emergencies, provides for the
efficient movement of motor vehicle and pedestrian traffic, trains and supervises school
crossing guards, animal control officer, public safety dispatchers, and the parking
enforcement officer. The Police Department over the past several years has committed to
the philosophy of community policing by forming alliances and partnerships with other
communities, community groups, instituting new programs and the use of technology.
Through an alliance with NEMLEC (North Eastern Massachusetts Law Enforcement
Council), the Police Department shares valuable resources with 59 other police
departments in northeastern Massachusetts. Locally, the Police Department works closely
with many community groups and organizations, such as the School Department and WCSC
(Winchester Coalition for a Safer Community through the Health Department). Community
Policing based programs include the STARS Programs (Safety in our schools) and our on-
going “Violence in the Work Place” education program for our local businesses and Town
departments. Through the use of our Web Page, Twitter, Facebook, the Department
continues to educate the community in crime prevention and public safety activities.

Unemployment Compensation
In 1976, unemployment compensation was extended by the Federal Government to State

can choose between two methods of payment (participation) of unemployment

reimbursement method. As a result, the Town is billed each month by the Department of
Unemployment Assistance for any claims attributable to the Town.

NON-APPROPRIATED CHARGES
MBTA
The MBTA provides rapid transit and other mass transit services to 175 cities and towns
including Winchester. Prior to the 1999 enactment of the reform package that overhauled
the budgeting and assessment procedures for the MBTA (commonly referred to as “forward
funding”), only 78 communities were assessed, totaling $145 million and increasing 2½%
annually. For purposes of determining each community’s assessment, the expenses are
broken down into two categories: Local and express service. Express service, which
basically involves rapid transit service, is assessed 75% by the commuter count (the number
of people who live in the town but work elsewhere), and 25% by the number of people
boarding in the community. The boarding count is taken for a one-week period each spring.
The local service is assessed 50% by the total deficit divided by the route miles in a
community. The MBTA is required by law to notify the State Treasurer of the amount of the
Net Assessable Cost of Service to be assessed to the municipalities within the district.

institutional facilities. Expenditures made for such purposes are assessed against the
metropolitan communities, one-half in proportion to the equalized valuations and one-half
by the population of each community.

considered to be general available revenues. Consequently, as part of the tax rate
preparation process, whatever amount is included within the State aid estimates is also
included in the non-appropriated expense section as offsetting debits. The Town receives
one grant annually for the public library. Tax Levy Overlay State law requires that the
Assessors put aside funds from each tax levy in a reserve that is called Overlay (also known
as Allowance for Abatements). This account is established in anticipation that a certain
percentage of the tax levy may end up being abated. Individual Undistributed IX-9 tax
abatements are paid out of this fund. The amount of the overlay account, the final value of

Program Description
The Winchester Recreation Department designs unique year-round and seasonal
recreational programs offered to all Winchester residents. Led by qualified instructors, our
programs offer participants affordable opportunities for a wide variety of passive and active
recreation, wellness, and leisure activities and oversight and coordination of the use and
permitting of all the towns fields, courts, and recreational areas.
The Recreation Department operates as an Enterprise Fund. Programs are self-supported
by setting user fees to cover all departmental expenses including indirect costs to other
town departments that support the department. The recreation department’s operating
budget funds all recreation expenses: staff, supplies and materials, professional services
needed to run the department such as cleaning of buildings, maintenance to facilities and
recreational areas, snow removal, burglar alarms and pest control. Surplus revenues
generated through the enterprise fund are logged into a retained earnings account that can
fund capital improvement projects, COLA increases, new recreation initiatives, budget
shortfalls or budget growth. Retained earnings expenditure can only be approved by an
affirmative vote at Annual Town Meetings.
An additional responsibility of the department is the management and scheduling of
athletic fields, court and open space rentals and reservations through two separate
revolving accounts, the Synthetic Field Account and the Grass Field Account. These
accounts are governed by town by laws and expenditure from the accounts must
specifically be listed in the bylaws. Expenditures are voted on and approved by the Field
Management Committee and must be signed off on by the Town Managers office for final
approval. The goal of these accounts is to reinvest funds back into fields, parks and sport
courts for general maintenance, improvements, and upkeep.

An Unusual Year
At first glance, the number of proposed projects may suggest a banner year for the Town. However, the
reality is more nuanced. Many of this year’s proposals are lower-cost projects such as improvements to
Mystic Playground, replacement of police Tasers and town voting machines, as well as restroom door
upgrades at athletic fields.
Unable to fund Town Hall HVAC
Despite the volume of projects, we are unable to fund our top-ranked building project: the Town Hall
HVAC system. Estimated at $8.5 million, this project will require funding from a source other than the
Building Stabilization Fund (BSF). Town Hall has not undergone a significant renovation in over 35 years.
The Committee strongly believes that this project—plus other high-cost, near-term Town Hall needs—
should be bundled into a comprehensive renovation package. Coordinating these projects together would
minimize both staff disruption (relocation will be required) and overall costs.
Planning for Symmes Corner

Capital Planning Process
The process for evaluating capital project requests begins in June of every year and
culminates at Fall Town Meeting. All elected and appointed Boards, Committees and school
and municipal departments are requested to submit next FY requests and projected 5 year
needs in order to create a comprehensive five-year needs assessment for the Town. This year
participation includes School Committee, Select Board, Town Manager, Energy Management
Committee, Disabilities Access Commission, and virtually all municipal department heads.
These requests are processed by Town staff, reviewed by Town management, and submitted
to the CPC for evaluation and prioritization.
The CPC consists of the following members per the Town’s charter. Two members appointed
by the Finance Committee, one member appointed by the Planning Board, one member
appointed by the School Committee and three members appointed by the Select Board. The
makeup of the CPC was designed to be inclusive of multiple viewpoints and relies on each
member representing an appointed Board or Committee to report back to said Committee
and also bring forward concerns and/or issues back to the CPC.
The CPC in evaluating each project relies on each Town entity submitting a proposal to take
into consideration their immediate and long-term capital needs. A major tool that is used by
the Town in developing our long-term immediate building repairs is the VFA/Gordian Building
Envelope Program. This program helps to develop the expected life cycle and cost estimates
for building system maintenance upgrade and replacement.

While the Town Manager is ultimately responsible for the town capital plan, through its active
participation and guidance in the prioritization process, Town management allows residents
to influence the capital process through its elected and appointed representatives. This
program allows for long term capital planning, which helps the Town prioritize its five-year
plan and align with the desires of town residents.
Additionally, the Department of Public Works and the Engineering Department have
developed a comprehensive program for sewer, water, drainage, roads, traffic, sidewalks and
flood mitigation projects. The Field Management Committee together with the DPW and

utilized by the various Town agencies contribute to the development of a comprehensive plan
for maintaining and improving the Town infrastructure and assets.
Project Evaluation Criteria
Fundamental to Capital Planning evaluation is a ranking process that allows the CPC to
evaluate each individual project in a consistent manner. The ranking is based on the following
criteria:
● Public Health and Safety
● Mandates by State or Federal Government
● Necessary for Maintenance of Town Assets
● Demonstrated Increased Efficiency and /or Cost Savings
Each project request is required to address the following items when submitting to the CPC.
1) Expected lifetime of the item/project.
2) Expected operating costs of and manpower available to complete or use the item or
project.
3) Comparison of purchasing versus leasing or outside contracting of the
service and, where relevant, the cost of multi-town sharing of resources or
equipment.
4) Need for the item/project and its effect on the operating budget-what it
might save in maintenance and repair.
5) Departmental priorities for the current year’s projects/items.
6) Cost estimates of the item/project, supported by bids or quotes whenever possible.
7) A plan detailing item/projects awarded during the past three (3) years and
those to be requested over the next three (3) years.
8) A schedule of implementation for approved items and projects, including a
timeline within the proposed FY.
