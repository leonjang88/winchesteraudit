Dear Board and Committee Members,
I am pleased to submit the Town Manager’s financial plan for Fiscal Year 2026 (July 1, 2025
through June 30, 2026), including municipal and school operating budgets, Recreation and Water &
Sewer Enterprise Funds, funded debt, and capital projects. The budget as proposed totals
$163,904,816 and was created with a commitment to maintaining level services.
In March 2019, the Town passed an override that included $8 million for operating expenses and $2
million for capital needs. When put forth to the voters, it was noted that the override would sustain
the school and municipal budgets for approximately two to three years, starting with the FY20
budget. The full $10 million capacity of the override was used to balance the FY22 budget, and since
that time staff and the Finance Committee have been able to make budget adjustments to extend the
override beyond its original projections, with FY26 representing the sixth year since the override
went into effect. In FY24, a reduction in the Town’s health insurance rates due to a change of
insurance providers allowed us to bridge the gap, resulting in a balanced budget. For FY25, the
budget was balanced through a combination of increases in state aid, budget reductions, and the use
of a modest amount of Free Cash ($262,859).
The current projected deficit for FY26 is significantly higher at $3,583,771, as growth in several key
budgetary areas has outpaced revenue growth. It is important to note that expense and revenue
projections will continue to evolve over the coming months as the Finance Committee prepares their
budget for presentation to Town Meeting. On the revenue side, the Manager’s budget makes use of
state aid and assessment numbers provided in the Governor’s budget, which was released in late
January. These numbers will continue to be refined as the state budget makes its way through the
House and Senate. On the expense side, the Town was notified two weeks ago of an 18.92% increase
to our health insurance costs for active employees. The Manager’s budget assumes a worst-case
scenario with the full amount of this increase; however, staff will be working over the coming
months to hopefully reduce these costs. Additionally, the School Committee has indicated that the
budget voted at their meeting on January 30th represents a “Preliminary Level Services Budget”, and
that they will be conducting additional public outreach ahead of a vote on their “FY26
Recommended Budget” on March 13th, which leaves uncertainty surrounding the 5.84% school
budget increase carried in the Manager’s budget.

FY26 Budget Development Process
Town staff began the FY26 budget process in Fall 2024. Municipal departmental budget requests
were submitted in early December and the Select Board convened a budget summit on January 10,
2025 with representatives from the Finance Committee, Capital Planning Committee, and School
Committee to discuss FY26 budget estimates, capital needs, and three-year budget model projections
for FY27 through FY29 to assess long-term trends and needs.
Revenue Projections
Total projected revenues for FY26 are $160,321,045. These projections were derived by increasing
the general tax levy by the allowable 2.5%, and projecting tax revenues from new construction (i.e.
“new growth”), estimating state aid, and projecting locally generated receipts such as motor vehicle
excise tax, meals tax, building permit fees, investment income, and other revenues. Additional details
are provided below.
• Property Taxes – Property tax remains the Town’s primary revenue source, comprising
approximately 65.3% of the total projected revenues in FY26. The Town’s property tax levy
is permitted to increase 2.5% over the prior year, plus the taxable value of new construction.
Prior year new growth becomes part of the general tax levy base upon which future 2.5%
increments are built. Based on the recommendation of the Director of Assessments, the new
growth projection for FY26 is $800,000, which is reduced from prior years (FY25 budget =
$1,059,299, FY24 actual = $1,041,723) as a result of an expected decrease in new
construction.
• State Aid – State aid for FY26 is projected to be $13,125,570, which represents an increase
of 3.3% over FY25, and is estimated at 8.2% of total FY26 revenues. This estimate is based
on preliminary numbers provided in Governor Healey’s FY26 budget dated January 22nd.
These estimates are subject to change as the legislative process unfolds, and should be
updated by the Finance Committee with best available information prior to Town Meeting.
• Local Receipts – Local receipt projections for FY26 represent an increase of 4.53% over the
FY25 budget, and reflect projected increases in motor vehicle excise tax, meals tax, penalties
and interest, and expected favorable investment income.
• Overlay – On January 24th, the Board of Assessor’s voted to release $400,000 from the
overlay account to the overlay surplus account. The Manager’s budget includes the use of
these funds as a source of revenue for the FY26 operating budget.
• Employee Healthcare and Health Insurance Stabilization Fund – The fund was
established at the Spring 2022 Town Meeting to set aside reserves to help offset unforeseen
increases in employee healthcare expenses. The Manager’s budget includes the use of
$125,000 from this fund.
• Closure of Unallocated Wage Accounts to the General Fund – Based on a recent ruling of
the Department of Revenue, the Town will now be required to close out Unallocated Wage
accounts annually, instead of allowing these accounts to carry over from year to year.
Therefore, at the end of each fiscal year, any unused funds will close directly to the General
Fund and ultimately become part of our certified Free Cash balance. This new practice will
begin immediately with the closure of the remaining funds in the FY23 and FY24 unallocated
wage accounts. The Manager’s budget includes the use of this closed-out balance ($257,414)
from Free Cash as a revenue source in FY26.
• Use of Reserves – In 2010, the Select Board adopted a formal Operating Reserves Policy,
which stated the Board’s objective to limit the amount of Free Cash used to balance the

operating budget to non-recurring expenditures, such as capital needs. The policy also lays

As noted above, the Town used the full capacity of the operating override passed in March
2019 to balance the FY22 budget. Beginning in FY23, the Town has a structural deficit
whereby the growth in revenues does not keep pace with the growth in costs necessary to
maintain a level service budget. This budget relies on the use of $3,583,771 in Free Cash to
close the gap for FY26. As currently projected, this deficit is expected to increase in the
three-year budget model developed for FY27 through FY29, and will require action in the out
years to ensure that the Town does not fall below the 6% minimum operating reserve
specified in the 2010 policy.
Expenditures
Projected total expenditures for FY26 are $163,904,816, including municipal and school budgets,
undistributed expenses, capital appropriations, non-appropriated expenses (state assessments, cherry
sheet offsets, and overlay), enterprise fund subsidies, and other expenses.
• Municipal Departments – Municipal departmental expenses, including Unallocated Wages,
are projected to increase 2.9% between FY25 and FY26, and comprise 21.6% of the total
FY26 expense budget. This budget reflects a level service program (inclusive of step
adjustments and wage increases), except for an increase in work hours for the building,
plumbing and electrical inspectors in the Building Department from 20 to 25 hours a week.
There is no change in benefit eligibility for these current employees.
• School Department – The FY26 budget allocation to the School Department is
$70,004,563, representing an increase of 5.84% over the FY25 budget, and comprising
42.7% of the Town’s total FY26 expense budget. As previously noted, the School
Committee has indicated that they will not vote their recommended budget until March 13th.
• Vocational Education – The FY26 budget reflects a 35.5% increase in vocational expenses
over the FY25 budget based on enrollment projections by the School Department for
students attending Northeast Metropolitan Technical High School (NEMT), Minuteman
Regional High School, Medford Vocational-Technical High School, and Essex North Shore
Agricultural & Technical School. The budget also reflects a capital assessment of $73,250 to
cover Winchester’s portion of the FY26 debt service related to the NEMT reconstruction
project.
• Undistributed – The “Undistributed” budget includes a number of expense categories that
are mandated, shared, or not otherwise allocated to the municipal or school budgets,
including energy costs, contributory retirement, worker’s compensation, unemployment
compensation, health insurance, Medicare tax, OPEB allocation, general insurance, reserve
fund, and environmental remediation services.

long-term permanent borrowings, and interest payments on short-term Bond Anticipation
Notes (BANs) issued by the Town, including estimates for General Obligation Bonds and
BANs that will be sold in March 2025. Staff will work with the Finance Committee to
finalize the debt budget after this sale.
Moody’s Bond Rating
The financial health of a community is reflected in part by the credit rating it earns when borrowing
funds. The Town’s Aaa bond rating was last reaffirmed by Moody’s in June 2024, which is the
highest rating available. In the most recent credit rating opinion, Moody’s noted that the Aaa rating
primarily reflects a strong economy that benefits from high resident income and wealth, a stable
financial position with improving liquidity and reserves, although levels remain below-average for
this highest rating category. Further, Moody’s identified the following factors that could lead to a
downgrading of the rating – decline in available reserves and/or liquidity as a percent of revenue,
material increase in leverage and/or fixed costs, and a significant decline in resident income, wealth,
or economic output.
Financial Outlook
The development of long-range financial projections for Winchester is key to understanding future
trends in revenues, expenses, and operating reserves over the coming years. As part of the FY26
budget development process, senior management developed a budget model for FY27 though FY29
and completed a sensitivity analysis to evaluate three scenarios representing various revenue growth
and expense projections. The model projects the operating reserves, including the use of Free Cash to
balance future year budgets, over the three-year planning horizon.

Conclusion
The long-term projections show that the structural deficit between revenue and expenses will grow
over the three-year planning horizon resulting in an unsustainable pattern of Free Cash usage and
reduction of the projected operating reserves below our 6% minimum threshold. It is critical that the
Town consider options in the near term to address this growing deficit. The Select Board has recently
indicated that they will reconstitute the State of the Town committee that served as advisors through
the previous override discussions in 2019. I look forward to working with the committee over the
coming months to develop a sustainable financial plan that supports the Town’s long-term capital
and operating needs.
Finally, I wish to extend my sincere thanks to Assistant Town Manager Mark Twogood, Comptroller
Stacie Ward, Jennifer Cafarella in the Town Manager’s Office, Superintendent Dr. Frank Hackett,
School Department Director of Finance & Operations Andrew Marron, our municipal Department
Heads, and members of the Select Board, School Committee, and Finance Committee for their work
during this budgeting process.

the Town Charter, first adopted in 1975. The Manager is responsible for the day-to-day operations of the

budget.

FINANCE COMMITTEE
Program Description
The Finance Committee consists of fifteen voters appointed for staggered three-year terms. Five
appointments are made annually by appointing committee consisting of the Moderator, and the Chairman
of the Select Board and Finance Committee respectively. Under the provision of the Town Charter and By-
Laws, the Finance Committee receives and reviews the proposed budget submitted by the Town Manager,
and after public hearings, submits a budget to the Town Meeting. The ongoing function of the Committee
is to act upon requests for “transfers”; i.e., appropriations from the Reserve Fund to meet extraordinary
and unforeseen needs of town departments and agencies which may arise during the course of the fiscal
year.

Program Description
The Comptroller is appointed by the Select Board and is responsible by Town Charter and State statute for
maintaining the general and budgetary accounting records of the Town, preparing the weekly payroll and
accounts payable warrants, preparing various financial reports of a statutory and/or administrative nature,
developing methods of installation and supervision of all accounting records of the Town, developing
standard practices relating to account matters, and the continuously reconciling, reviewing and auditing
account balances and activity. The Comptroller provides budget and forecast planning support during the
budget development process and as needed.
The Comptroller’s Office administers the unemployment activity and the general insurance program which
includes property, liability (and excess liability), public official, flood, vacancy and other coverage.
In addition to the day-to-day duties and responsibilities, the Comptroller’s Office is responsible for
preparing employee W-2’s, vendor 1099’s and Affordable Care Act 1095c forms annually. The
Comptroller’s Office also coordinates the annual financial statement audit, which includes providing a
myriad of supporting documentation and analysis and coordinates the Other Post-Employment Benefits
(OPEB) actuarial studies and roll-forwards, which includes preparing complex insurance and retiree census
data.
The Comptroller is the ex-officio voting member of the Winchester Contributory Retirement Board, an ex-
officio non-voting member of the Commissioners of Trust Funds and an ex-officio non-voting member of
the Town’s Retiree Health Insurance Advisory Committee.

AUDIT
Program Description
The Audit budget provides funding for the annual financial statement audit performed in accordance with
generally accepted auditing standards and standards applicable to financial audits contained in
Government Auditing Standards. A financial statement audit is an examination of the Town’s books and
records and includes the review of accounting transactions, policies, procedures, and internal controls. The
Town is required to obtain a financial statement audit due to the level of federal funds expended and due
to bond disclosure requirements.
The Select Board established an Audit Advisory Committee in 2006 to assist them in their oversight of the
annual financial statement audit. The Audit Advisory Committee facilitates communication between the
Select Board, the audit firm and town management. They also make recommendations related to the
selection of the audit firm and review the financial statements and other audit reports regarding financial
management issues. The Comptroller is responsible for the day-to-day management of the audit process
and works closely with the audit firm. Fiscal year 2026 is the third year of a three-year contract with
CliftonLarsonAllen LLP with a cost of $90,000.
The audit budget also provides funding for a biennial Other Post Employment Benefit (OPEB) actuarial
valuation and consulting services. The Town currently provides postemployment benefits including
medical, dental and life insurance to retirees and their dependents. The actuarial valuation is required to
provide the necessary data associated with these benefits to comply with financial statement reporting
and disclosure requirements in accordance with Governmental Accounting Standards Board Statements
No.74 & 75. There will not be an OPEB actuarial valuation performed in FY26, therefore there are no funds
being added for these services in the FY26 audit budget.

Program Description
The Conservation Commission is appointed by the Town Manager and is responsible for protecting the
Town’s natural resources. The Conservation Commission manages over 130+ acres of open space,
dedicated to passive recreation and to the preservation of wildlife habitat. These areas give quiet refuge
and an opportunity to experience nature and serve as habitat for wildlife. We do this on a small budget,
making use of much volunteer help, both with Commission members and volunteers who donate their
time to clear passages, build crossings, construct signs, and blaze trails. With each permit issued, we
educate landowners in the importance of food and cover to native and migrating species and require
them to landscape for their preservation. We are actively involved in promoting clean water, and work
to ensure that discharges into waterbodies are free of pollutants. We have watched waterfowl and fish
return to our waterways, the return of migrating herring, and the return of apex species - indicative of a
healthy ecosystem. In the long term, it is these preserved areas, and the efforts of educated
landowners, that will contribute to flood resilience, drought control, and a quality of life that
incorporates nature and its many benefits.

primarily to health maintenance, case management, food insecurity, nutrition and dining, transportation
services, educational programs and events, information and referral to access resources. A majority of

Program Description
The Police Department’s primary function is the enforcement of laws and protection of life and
property. This includes prevention of crime, preservation of the peace, law enforcement and detection
of violators. When law violators are detected, they can be cited, summonsed or arrested. In the case of
juveniles and young adults, when appropriate the police department can utilize various diversion
programs to prevent a juvenile or young adult from having a criminal record. The Police Department
responds to emergencies, provides for the efficient movement of motor vehicle and pedestrian traffic,
trains and supervises school crossing guards, animal control officer, public safety dispatchers, and the
parking enforcement officer. The Police Department over the past several years has committed to the
philosophy of community policing by forming alliances and partnerships with other communities,
community groups, instituting new programs and the use of technology. Through an alliance with
NEMLEC (North Eastern Massachusetts Law Enforcement Council), the Police Department shares
valuable resources with 59 other police departments in northeastern Massachusetts. Locally, the Police
Department works closely with many community groups and organizations, such as the School
Department and WCSC (Winchester Coalition for a Safer Community through the Health Department).
Community Policing based programs include the STARS Programs (Safety in our schools) and our on-
going “Violence in the Work Place” education program for our local businesses and Town departments.
Through the use of our Web Page, Twitter, Facebook, the Department continues to educate the
community in crime prevention and public safety activities.

ensuring that these services continue uninterrupted. Transportation costs have been adjusted to
accommodate the opening of the new Lynch Elementary School.

funding for out-of-district placements in the general fund was increased to accommodate higher
enrollments, state-approved tuition rate hikes averaging approximately 7%, and the expiration of ESSER

What is a Level Service Budget?
A Level Services budget is defined as taking the current year’s level of services and projecting that same level of services
forward one year.
There are many factors that are considered when projecting forward one year including enrollment changes, rate changes for
expenses such as utilities or insurances, staff turnover, retirements and contracted salary increases.
What is a Recommended Budget?
Our recommended budget will build on the Level Services Budget to include those resources (people, materials,
etc) needed to support our teachers and students. This budget will include updated numbers to reflect changes that
may occur over the next several weeks, including enrollment projections, special service needs, and other items
that may not be included in the Level Services Budget.
Most importantly, it will include priorities identified through engagement with our teachers, staff, parents, and
broader community. The Recommended Budget will be designed to align with the considerations from our
Committee and Leadership to meet our Strategic Goals, as determined by both staff and the community.

ENERGY
The Energy budget is a consolidated account that funds all energy cost for municipal and school
departments. It includes the cost of heating oil, natural gas, and electricity, as well as gasoline and diesel
fuel for Town vehicles.
The Energy Management Committee (EMC), formed in 2006, has worked with town boards and
departments to implement energy saving measures. EMC initiatives have resulted in over $7 million in
avoided costs cumulative since the Committee’s inception. The Town has also created an Energy
Revolving Fund to provide financial support for energy conservation projects along with utility incentives
and grant funding. Additionally, Winchester is a member of the Green Community program, and our
goal is to save an additional 20% in energy usage. In the past, solar arrays have been installed on the
roofs of the Vinson-Owen School, DPW, and Winchester High School to reduce our energy expenses at
these locations.
During FY25, the following energy projects will be on-going or completed:
• Mystic School heat repairs and replacements to transition a portion of the building from steam
heat to VRF heat pumps, which was funded through a combination of ARPA funds and Free
Cash; and
• Mystic School window replacement, which was funded in part through a grant from the
Massachusetts Department of Energy Resources (DOER).
The new Lynch Elementary School is scheduled to open in September 2025. The building will be all
electric and equipped with roof and ground-mounted solar panels to achieve Net Zero status. Since
operating costs for the new building are currently unknown, staff has assumed $80,000 in energy costs
for the Lynch School. The budget also assumes a return to prior usage amounts at the Parkhurst School,
which had been temporarily used for swing space for displaced Lynch students.

guidance, senior management used the 10% renewal number for the purposes of preliminary budget

MBTA
The MBTA provides rapid transit and other mass transit services to 175 cities and towns including
Winchester. Prior to the 1999 enactment of the reform package that overhauled the budgeting and
assessment procedures for the MBTA (commonly referred to as “forward funding”), only 78
communities were assessed, totaling $145 million and increasing 2½% annually. For purposes of
determining each community’s assessment, the expenses are broken down into two categories: Local
and express service. Express service, which basically involves rapid transit service, is assessed 75% by the
commuter count (the number of people who live in the town but work elsewhere), and 25% by the
number of people boarding in the community. The boarding count is taken for a one-week period each
spring. The local service is assessed 50% by the total deficit divided by the route miles in a community.
The MBTA is required by law to notify the State Treasurer of the amount of the Net Assessable Cost of
Service to be assessed to the municipalities within the district.
Registry of Motor Vehicles Non-Renewal Surcharge
If after proper notices, a motorist fails to pay a parking fine, motor vehicle excise tax, or a charge for
abandonment of a motor vehicle, the Town notifies the Registrar of Motor Vehicles (RMV) not to renew
the license and registration of that motorist. To cover the RMV’s administrative costs of entering the
necessary information into its computer system, the RMV assesses the Town a fee of $20 for each
notification it receives. This fee, which comes through as a charge on the Cherry Sheet, is recovered by
the Town by adding this amount and other penalties to the original fine print.
Metropolitan Area Planning Council
The Metropolitan Area Planning Council (MAPC) is a regional planning agency representing 101 cities
and towns in the metropolitan Boston area. Created by an act of the Legislature in 1963, it serves as an
independent public body of the Commonwealth with which state and local officials can address issues of
regional importance.
Air Pollution Control District
General Laws Chapter 11, Section 142B, requires that communities be assessed for a portion of the costs
incurred by the State Department of Environmental Protection (DEP) to monitor air pollution levels and
enforce air quality standards at industrial, commercial, and institutional facilities. Expenditures made for
such purposes are assessed against the metropolitan communities, one-half in proportion to the
equalized valuations and one-half by the population of each community.
Cherry Sheet Offsets
Included in the estimated amount of aid to be received from the State are grant funds for the library.
These funds are reserved for direct expenditure by the departments and are not considered to be
general available revenues. Consequently, as part of the tax rate preparation process, whatever amount
is included within the State aid estimates is also included in the non-appropriated expense section as
offsetting debits. The Town receives one grant annually for the public library.

Program Description
The Winchester Recreation Department designs unique year-round and seasonal recreational programs
offered to all Winchester residents. Led by qualified instructors, our programs offer participants
affordable opportunities for a wide variety of passive and active recreation, wellness, and leisure
activities and oversight and coordination of the use and permitting of all the towns fields, courts, and
recreational areas.
The Recreation Department operates as an Enterprise Fund. Programs are self-supported by setting user
fees to cover all departmental expenses including indirect costs to other town departments that support
the department. The recreation department’s operating budget funds all recreation expenses: staff,
supplies and materials, professional services needed to run the department such as cleaning of
buildings, maintenance to facilities and recreational areas, snow removal, burglar alarms and pest
control. Surplus revenues generated through the enterprise fund are logged into a retained earnings
account that can fund capital improvement projects, COLA increases, new recreation initiatives budget
shortfalls or budget growth. Retained earnings expenditure can only be approved by an affirmative vote
at Annual Town Meetings.
An additional responsibility of the department is the management and scheduling of athletic fields,
court and open space rentals and reservations through two separate revolving accounts, the Synthetic
Field Account and the Grass Field Account. These accounts are governed by town by laws and
expenditure from the accounts must specifically be listed in the bylaws. Expenditures are voted on and
approved by the Field Management Committee and must be signed off on by the Town Managers office
for final approval. The goal of these accounts is to reinvest funds back into fields, parks and sport courts
for general maintenance, improvements, and upkeep.

The Good News…
From Outside the Town
Repairs to the South Reservoir dam have begun and it is with great delight that the Capital Planning
Committee (CPC) can report that town has received a $1 million grant from the Executive Office of
Energy and Environmental Affairs’ (EEA) Dam and Seawall program.
Also, with the assistance of Representative Day and Senator Lewis, the Town has received a
$100,000 grant from the State toward the purchase a Trommel screener for the transfer station.
CPC is proposing to match that grant for the purchase of that equipment. The town currently rents
that equipment each year for about $20,000 so payback will be swift.
From Inside the Town
Last year the Capital Planning Committee (CPC) recommended the funding of engineering for
required roof, window, and HVAC repairs to Schools and Municipal buildings that would lead to
construction projects estimated to cost nearly
$10 million. This year, the combination of having a large project completing its 20-year debt payoff
(allowing a responsible amount of borrowing), larger than normal balances in the Funds (robust
investment and interest income, and substantial project closeouts), and a large certified Free Cash
Balance, has allowed the CPC, working closely with the Select Board, to recommend nearly $10
million in building projects and $3.2 million in capital projects this year. The building projects will be
funded with $3.8 million from the Building Stabilization Fund (BSF), $4 million in new bonding, and
$2 million in Free Cash. The capital projects will be funded with $3 million from the Capital
Stabilization Fund (CSF) and $200,000 in Free Cash.
This level of funding will enable the Town to undertake the urgently needed replacement of sections
of the McCall Middle School roof and initiate repairs to the nearly 20-year old Ambrose School roof.
CPC, with the EFPBC, will recommend repairs and treatment to the High School concrete slab floor to
prevent further deterioration and to affect repairs of the slab in limited areas.

Challenges Remain (we are not out of the woods)
CPC has completed its evaluation of capital project requests submitted by Town Departments for the
next 5 years and the outlook remains disturbing. That review, which now includes projects being
generated by the Town’s VFA system, shows nearly $45 million in building projects and $36 million in
capital projects over the next 5 years.
While great strides will be made this year, this year’s review of capital projects started with over $25
million in building projects this cycle alone. There are nearly $17.5 million in building projects for
which the town now has complete or nearly complete engineering designs. These projects, while
important and worthy of funding, were considered of lower priority or beyond the reach of funding
at this time.
Over the next 3 capital cycles, the town will need to replace nearly $2.5 million in Fire Department
equipment. Given the 1 to 2 year lead time for Fire Pumpers and Ladder Trucks, CPC has begun
reserving CSF funds for that equipment. Even so, those required expenses will place considerable
stress on the CSF in those years.
The stabilization funds alone are still unable to support the number of projects of these
magnitudes and alternate sources of funding (Override, Free Cash, etc.) will be required. CPC will
continue to work closely with the Select Board seeking Free Cash when available, responsible
bonding when appropriate, and debt exclusions when required.

BUILDING STABILIZATION FUND
Town Hall HVAC Immediate Repairs
The Town Hall HVAC system was installed in the late 1980s and is at the end of its useful life.
There are many components of the system that must be replaced on a large scale, as the
complete replacement of the system has not been funded. The focus of this appropriation will be
on the replacement of components that are failing in order to keep the operational. It will not
address the issue of a lack of fresh air which is causing other concerning issues in the lower level
of the building. Until the HVAC system is replaced this request will be made annually to keep the
system up and running.
DPW Complex Emergency Generator
This request will fund a new emergency generator for the DPW complex. The existing generator,
which is over 50 years old, has failed and cannot be repaired under existing code requirements.
High School Concrete Floor Construction
This request will fund the initial phase of repair and remediation of failing concrete and
reinforcing steel at Winchester High School. This first phase of repair will include the application
of a surface applied corrosion inhibitor to the underside of the first-floor slab, which can take
place during the school year. The second phase of repairs, which we hope to have ready for
Spring Town Meeting, will involve selective demolition, repair and reconstruction to limited areas
of the topside of the first-floor concrete. This fall appropriation includes additional inspection,
design and engineering as well as cost estimates for the subsequent topside work so that a Spring
Town Meeting appropriation will permit summer repairs.
Town Hall Roof & Clock Tower / Library Roof & Windows
This request will fund the final design and the repairs/replacement for the Town Hall Roof and
clock Tower and the Library Roof and original windows. Town Meeting previously appropriated
funds for schematic level design and engineering, as well as immediate repairs to the library roof
to address active leaks. This request funds the completion of the project.
McCall Middle School and Ambrose School Roofs
This request will fund the final design and the repairs/replacement of older portions of the McCall
Middle School roofs and Ambrose Elementary School Roof. Town Meeting previously
appropriated funds for schematic level design and engineering. This request funds the
construction portion of the project.

School District Access Controls
This request will fund the replacement of the district wide access control system. The current
system limits the district’s ability to implement security updates and integrate with modern
technologies. By replacing the system district-wide, the focus is to implement an interoperable
system, utilizing interchangeable hardware and software. This approach ensures compatibility
and flexibility for future upgrades and expansions. The timing of this upgrade is critical, as it aligns
with the construction and opening of the new Lynch Elementary School. This allows the entire
district to be equipped with the same standard that the New Lynch is being built to along with
new access ID cards before school opens in 2025. This comprehensive upgrade will enhance the
overall security and safety of the district's facilities, providing a modern and reliable access
control infrastructure for all schools.

This request will fund the replacement of older security cameras at Winchester High School and
McCall Middle School. The existing cameras are either malfunctioning, offline or in need of
replacement due to age.
Ginn Field Lights
This request will supplement the appropriation previously made by Town Meeting for the
replacement of the Ginn Field Athletic Light fixtures and poles. The current lights are 30 years old
and have many deficiencies. The new lights will utilize LED Lamps which provide better and more
efficient lighting, and less spill over to nearby residents and less glare. After completing a rigorous
design and permitting process the final estimates exceeded the balance of the original
appropriation. This request funds the completion of the project.
Packer Ellis Tennis Courts Engineering
This request will fund the next phase of design and engineering of the rehabilitation of the Packer-
Ellis Tennis Court Facility. The facility is over 100 years old and is well loved by all who use it, but
has many deficiencies that must be addressed. It is utilized by more than 1500 people a year
including the Winchester High School Tennis teams. Town Meeting previously appropriated
funding for the schematic design phase and this appropriation will allow the project to move into
the design development phase.

Capital Planning Process
The process for evaluating capital project requests begins in June of every year and culminates at
Fall Town Meeting. All elected and appointed Boards, Committees and school and municipal
departments are requested to submit next FY requests and projected 5 year needs in order to
create a comprehensive five- year needs assessment for the Town. This year participation
includes School Committee, Select Board, Town Manager, Energy Management Committee,
Disabilities Access Commission, and virtually all municipal department heads. These requests are
processed by Town staff, reviewed by Town management, and submitted to the CPC for
evaluation and prioritization.
The CPC consists of the following members per the Town’s charter. Two members appointed by
the Finance Committee, one member appointed by the Planning Board, one member appointed
by the School Committee and three members appointed by the Select Board. The makeup of the
CPC was designed to be inclusive of multiple viewpoints and relies on each member representing
an appointed Board or Committee to report back to said Committee and also bring forward
concerns and/or issues back to the CPC.
The CPC in evaluating each project relies on each Town entity submitting a proposal to take into
consideration their immediate and long-term capital needs. A major tool that is used by the Town
in developing our long-term immediate building repairs is the VFA/Gordian Building Envelope
Program. This program helps to develop the expected life cycle and cost estimates for building
system maintenance upgrade and replacement.
While the Town Manager is ultimately responsible for the town capital plan, through its active
participation and guidance in the prioritization process, Town management allows residents to
influence the capital process through its elected and appointed representatives. This program
allows for long term capital planning which helps the Town prioritize its five-year plan and align
with the desires of town residents.
Additionally, the Department of Public Works and the Engineering Department have
developed a comprehensive program for sewer, water, drainage, roads, traffic,
sidewalks and flood mitigation projects. The Field Management Committee together
with the DPW and School Department have developed a five- year field
improvements plan. All of these tools utilized by the various Town agencies
contribute to the development of a comprehensive plan for maintaining and
improving the Town infrastructure and assets.
Project Evaluation Criteria
Fundamental to Capital Planning evaluation is a ranking process that allows the CPC to evaluate
each individual project in a consistent manner. The ranking is based on the following criteria:
● Public Health and Safety
● Mandates by State or Federal Government
● Necessary for Maintenance of Town Assets
● Demonstrated Increased Efficiency and /or Cost Savings
Each project request is required to address the following items when submitting to the CPC.
1) Expected lifetime of the item/project.

2) Expected operating costs of and manpower available to complete or use the item

3) Comparison of purchasing versus leasing or outside contracting of the service and,

4) Need for the item/project and its effect on the operating budget-what it might

8) A schedule of implementation for approved items and projects, including a

One of the critical steps in completing the CPC’s evaluation of each project is meeting individually
with department representatives seeking project funding. In these meetings we discuss where
the request fits into the overall goals for departmental long-range capital plans. In this process
each party submitting a request is asked to ensure that alternative strategies for meeting their
needs have been considered. It is also required that each party has evaluated utilizing alternative
funding sources as well, if available.
