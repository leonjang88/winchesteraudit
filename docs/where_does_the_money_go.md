# Where Does Winchester's Money Go?

**The core question:** Winchester collects roughly the same revenue per capita as Wellesley, Needham, and Concord — but spends significantly less on education. Where does the money go instead?

---

## The Short Answer

Winchester's education gap is **84% explained by two structural factors:**

1. **Lower property tax base** — Winchester collects $5.7M less in tax levy than similar-revenue towns, despite similar tax rates. The assessed value per capita ($497,829) is below Wellesley ($570,479) and Concord ($518,618).

2. **DPW absorbs facilities costs** — Winchester spends $4.1M more on DPW than comparable towns because custodians ($1.9M) and building maintenance ($540K) are budgeted under DPW, not a separate Facilities department.

**Winchester is not "wasting" money on the wrong priorities.** The town actually runs lean administration and mid-range public safety. The education gap is structural — lower tax base + different budget classification — not a spending choice.

## Revenue Comparison: Towns With Similar Per-Capita Revenue

| Town | Revenue/Capita | Tax Levy/Cap | State Aid/Cap | Other/Cap |
|------|---------------|-------------|-------------|----------|
| Concord | $7,758 | $6,427 | $371 | $960 |
| Needham | $7,678 | $5,984 | $503 | $1,191 |
| Wellesley | $7,245 | $5,865 | $394 | $986 |
| **Winchester** | **$7,238** | **$5,508** | **$556** | **$1,174** |

Winchester's total revenue per capita is essentially identical to Wellesley. But the tax levy component is $357 lower per capita ($5,508 vs $5,865) — that's **$8.2M less** from property taxes alone.

### Why Is the Levy Lower?

| Factor | Winchester | Wellesley | Gap |
|--------|-----------|----------|-----|
| Assessed Value/Capita | $497,829 | $570,479 | -$72,650 |
| Residential Tax Rate | $11.09 | $10.28 | +$0.81 |
| Total Tax Levy | $125.8M | $180.3M | -$54.5M |

Winchester has a **higher tax rate** but **lower assessed values**. The rate can't fully compensate for the property value gap. Winchester's homes are worth less per capita than Wellesley's, so even taxing harder produces less revenue.

## Accounting for the $11.7M Education Gap

| Factor | Amount | % of Gap |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Lower tax levy (lower assessed values) | -$5.7M | 49% |
| DPW facilities costs (custodians + bldg maint) | -$4.1M | 35% |
| Lower human services spending | -$0.7M | 6% |
| Lower gen government spending | -$1.9M | 16% |
| **Subtotal accounted for** | **-$12.4M** | **106%** |

> The gap is fully explained. Winchester doesn't have a spending priorities problem — it has a **revenue structure** problem (lower AV) and a **budget classification** problem (DPW includes facilities).

## The Classification Problem: DPW = DPW + Facilities

| Winchester DPW Item | Amount | In Other Towns |
|--------------------|--------|---------------|
| Custodians (bldg maintenance staff) | $1,906,122 | Facilities Dept |
| R&M Buildings & Structures | $540,000 | Facilities Dept |

| Solid Waste Disposal | $971,190 | Some towns: Enterprise Fund |
| Recycling Services | $385,000 | Some towns: Enterprise Fund |
| **Subtotal "Potentially Enterprise"** | **$1,356,190** | |

If Winchester:
1. Moved facilities to a separate department: DPW drops by $2.4M
2. Moved solid waste to enterprise fund: DPW drops by another $1.4M
3. **Total DPW reduction: $3.8M** — bringing Winchester's DPW in line with comps

None of this changes total spending — it just reclassifies it. But it would make the budget more transparent and the DPW comparison more accurate.

## The Percentage Question

The dollar analysis above explains why Winchester has fewer dollars for education. But there's a separate question: **even with the dollars it has, why does Winchester allocate a lower percentage to education?**

Winchester puts 46.7% of its budget toward education — 8th of 9. Even towns with similar or lower total budgets (Belmont, Arlington) allocate more.

| Belmont | 47.8% | 52.2% | Debt 11.1%, GG 10.6%, PS 10.9% |

DPW alone covers the gap. But as we showed in the DPW analysis, $2.4M of that is facilities costs that other towns budget elsewhere — it's classification, not waste.

### What Every Comp Town Trades Off for Higher Education

**Every town** that allocates more to education than Winchester does it by spending less on DPW:

| Town | Ed % | How They Fund It (vs Winchester) |
|------|------|--------------------------------|
| **Concord** | 56.4% | DPW -4.2pp, Fixed Costs -6.7pp, Debt -1.0pp |
| **Lexington** | 52.3% | DPW -4.6pp, Police+Fire -2.9pp, Debt -0.9pp |
| **Belmont** | 47.8% | DPW -2.7pp, Fixed Costs -7.1pp |
| **Needham** | 47.8% | DPW -3.7pp, GenGov -1.2pp |
| **Arlington** | 47.6% | DPW -2.7pp, GenGov -2.6pp, Police+Fire -1.0pp |
| **Wellesley** | 47.3% | DPW -2.2pp, Police+Fire -1.8pp |

**The universal pattern: Lower DPW = higher education.** The second lever varies:
- Concord + Belmont: much lower Fixed Costs (different insurance structure)
- Lexington: lower Police+Fire (economies of scale from larger population)
- Arlington: lower GenGov (also economies of scale)

### The Combined Answer

Winchester's education underfunding has **two layers:**

**Layer 1 — Fewer dollars (the revenue gap):**
- Lower assessed values = $5.7M less tax levy than similar-revenue towns
- This means fewer total dollars to allocate, regardless of priorities

**Layer 2 — Lower percentage (the allocation gap):**
- Winchester allocates 46.7% to education vs 48.8% average
- DPW at 8.3% (vs 6.2% average) absorbs 2.0pp that could go to education
- $2.4M of that DPW excess is facilities costs classified differently
- Police+Fire at 10.1% (vs 9.1%) absorbs another 1.0pp

**Both layers matter.** Even if Winchester reallocated from DPW to education, it would only reach 48.8% — still below Concord (56.4%) and Lexington (52.3%). To truly match those towns requires both reallocation AND more revenue (via override or assessed value growth).

---

## Can Reallocation Close the Gap?

**No.** The per-student gap to the comp average is $6,834 ($29.7M total). Here's what each lever provides:

| Scenario | $ Moved | New Per Student | Gap Closed | Rank |
|---------|---------|----------------|-----------|------|
| Current (FY24 DLS) | — | $14,915 | — | #8 of 9 |
| Move DPW excess only | $2.8M | $15,557 | 9% | #7 |
| Move DPW + Police/Fire excess | $4.2M | $15,883 | 14% | #7 |
| Move ALL excess (DPW+PS+Fixed) | $5.4M | $16,156 | 18% | #7 |
| FY27 Override alone | $11.5M | $17,570 | 39% | #7 |
| **Override + ALL reallocation** | **$16.9M** | **$18,811** | **57%** | **#6** |
| To match comp average | $29.7M | $21,749 | 100% | #5 |
| To match Lexington | $27.5M | $21,242 | — | — |

Even the most aggressive scenario (override + moving every dollar of excess from three departments) only closes 57% of the gap and moves Winchester from #8 to #6.

**The remaining $12.8M gap ($2,938/student) can only be closed by growing revenue** — either through assessed value growth (new construction, property appreciation) or additional overrides.

---

## Conclusions

1. **Winchester is not mismanaging money.** The education gap is structural — lower property values and DPW classification — not bad choices.

2. **The DPW "overspend" is mostly accounting.** Moving facilities and solid waste to their own categories would make Winchester look normal.

3. **The real constraint is assessed values.** Winchester's property values per capita are 5th of 8 comp towns. You can't tax what you don't have.

4. **The override is the right tool.** Since the levy is at the Prop 2½ ceiling, an override is the only way to increase education spending in the short term.

5. **Health insurance is the controllable cost.** At $825/capita (highest of comps), health insurance is where Winchester has room to find efficiencies — especially school employee coverage at $9.3M.

6. **Long-term, new growth matters most.** Towns that grow their tax base through development can fund education increases without overrides. Winchester's new growth rate relative to comps is worth investigating.
