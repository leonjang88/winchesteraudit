# Benefits & Fixed Costs Analysis: The Classification Problem

**Finding from DLS:** Concord and Belmont appear to spend dramatically less on Fixed Costs (8.9% and 8.5% of budget vs Winchester's 15.6%). This looked like a massive efficiency difference.

**Reality:** It's mostly a **classification difference**, not a spending difference.

---

## The Misleading DLS Comparison

| Town | DLS "Fixed Costs" % | Looks Like |
|------|-------------------|-----------|
| Weston | 18.5% | Very high |
| Arlington | 18.2% | Very high |
| Needham | 18.0% | Very high |
| Winchester | 15.6% | Average |
| Concord | 8.9% | **Way low** |
| Belmont | 8.5% | **Way low** |

This suggested Concord and Belmont had found a way to dramatically reduce benefits costs. **They haven't.**

---

Winchester actually has the **highest per-capita health insurance costs** of all comp towns. This is hidden in the DLS data because DLS categorizes items differently across towns.

## Why Concord's DLS "Fixed Costs" Looks Low

Concord bundles **$9.6M in Debt Service** into their "Joint Accounts" section alongside benefits. DLS classifies Debt Service separately from Fixed Costs. So Concord's benefits spending *appears* lower in DLS because some of it lands in the Debt Service category instead.

### Concord FY27 "Joint Accounts" (what they call benefits)

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Group Insurance | $10,097,630 |
| Retirement Assessment (Gen Fund) | $3,727,523 |
| Retirement Assessment (Pension Reserve) | $1,618,041 |
| **Debt Service (within levy)** | **$3,955,051** |
| **Debt Service (excluded)** | **$937,851** |
| **DPS Principal & Interest** | **$7,653,884** |
| Property/Liability Insurance | $701,151 |
| Workers Comp | $276,175 |
| Medicare | $941,379 |
| Unemployment | $143,575 |
| **Total Joint Accounts** | **$30,810,606** |

Of that $30.8M, **$12.5M is debt service** — which DLS puts in a different category. Strip debt out and Concord's benefits are $18.3M, or $1,012/capita. Not dramatically different from Winchester.

## The Real Question

The DLS comparison made it look like Concord and Belmont had cracked the code on benefits. They haven't — it's classification noise. 

**Winchester's actual benefits issue is health insurance at $825/capita** — highest of the comp towns. The question isn't "how does Concord spend less on fixed costs?" It's "why does Winchester pay more per capita for health insurance than any comp town?"

Possible drivers:
1. **Plan richness** — Winchester may offer more generous health plans
2. **GIC membership** — some towns joined the Group Insurance Commission for lower rates
3. **Employee/retiree ratio** — Winchester may have more retirees per active employee
4. **Cost-sharing** — the employee contribution % may be lower than comp towns
5. **School staffing intensity** — school health insurance ($9.3M) is nearly half the total

---

## Recommendations

1. **Don't use DLS "Fixed Costs" for cross-town comparison** — the classification is inconsistent. Use the detailed benefits data instead.
2. **Investigate health insurance plan design** — compare plan types, employee contribution rates, and GIC membership across comp towns
3. **Benchmark retiree health costs** — Winchester's $2.3M for school retirees is worth comparing
4. **School health insurance** — $9.3M for school employees is the elephant in the room. How does this compare per-teacher to comp towns?
